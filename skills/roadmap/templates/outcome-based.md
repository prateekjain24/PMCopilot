## Roadmap Structure

This roadmap is organized as a four-level hierarchy:

```
Outcome (the change we want to see in the world)
  Key Result (how we measure progress toward the outcome)
    Initiative (the work we believe will drive the key result)
      Metric (how we track whether the initiative is working)
```

The critical distinction: **outcomes describe the destination, not the vehicle.** Initiatives can change if we discover a better path to the same outcome.

## Guidance: Writing Good Outcomes

### Outcomes should be user-centric or business-centric, not feature-centric

**Bad outcomes (feature-centric):**
- "Launch the new dashboard"
- "Migrate to microservices"
- "Add SSO support"
- "Build a mobile app"

These describe solutions, not the change you want to create. If you name a feature, you have already locked in the solution before validating the problem.

**Good outcomes (user/business-centric):**
- "Users can find the information they need in under 30 seconds"
- "Engineering teams ship independently without cross-team coordination delays"
- "Enterprise customers can meet their security and compliance requirements without workarounds"
- "Users accomplish their core tasks regardless of which device they are using"

### Characteristics of a well-written outcome

1. **Describes a change in state**, not an activity. "Improve retention" is a direction. "New users return for a second session within 7 days at a rate of 60%" is a measurable outcome.
2. **Is measurable.** You should be able to answer "did we achieve this?" with data, not opinions.
3. **Is achievable within the time horizon.** Avoid outcomes so large they span multiple years unless the roadmap explicitly covers that period.
4. **Is within your sphere of influence.** The team should be able to meaningfully move the needle through their own work, even if external factors also play a role.
5. **Leaves room for multiple solutions.** If only one initiative could possibly achieve the outcome, the outcome is likely too narrow (it is a disguised feature).

### The litmus test

Ask: "If we achieved this outcome through a completely different set of initiatives than the ones listed, would we still be happy?" If the answer is yes, the outcome is well-written. If the answer is no, you have likely written a feature dressed up as an outcome.

## Review Cadence

- **Monthly:** Review metrics for each initiative. Assess whether initiatives are moving their key results. Pivot or stop initiatives that are not showing signal.
- **Quarterly:** Full roadmap review. Evaluate whether outcomes are still the right ones given updated strategy and market conditions. Retire achieved outcomes and introduce new ones.
- **Ad hoc:** When significant new information arrives (competitor move, user research insight, leadership strategy shift), reassess whether the outcome hierarchy still holds.
